Part I
The Diagnosis

What the April program and the last 30 days of Shopify data actually show — and why the honest read is more hopeful than the headline number.

Methodology

How we read the data

We pulled six Shopify reports for the last 30 days (April 14 – May 14, 2026), each filtered against Shopify's native human-or-bot session classifier:

  • Sessions by location — country, region, city
  • Sessions by traffic source — Direct, Search, Social, Referral
  • Sessions by device, by landing page, and by referring URL
  • The conversion funnel by source — sessions → add-to-cart → checkout → order

We chose the 30-day window over the full history deliberately: earlier sessions are heavily contaminated by founder and team test orders and pre-launch QA traffic. Thirty days gives the cleanest view of how the live program actually performs.

One limitation, stated plainly

We did not have Meta Ads Manager access during this analysis — the previous agency has not yet shared it. Several conclusions below are forensic inferences from funnel signatures. They would be confirmed, or sharpened, with direct Meta access — which is why requesting it is the first action in the plan.

Finding 01

19.3% of traffic is non-human — and it is not the problem

Of 5,545 raw sessions in the last 30 days, 1,069 (19.3%) were non-human. The instinct is to call this a bot problem. The data says otherwise — the signature is unusually clean, and it points to passive crawlers, not paid junk traffic.

81.5% of bot sessions are desktop; 68.6% of human sessions are mobile. That inversion is the signature of web scrapers, SEO tools, competitor monitoring and AI training crawlers — Common Crawl, GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, Bytespider — none of which cost money to acquire.

These crawlers inflate the raw session count by roughly 19%, which artificially depresses the headline conversion rate — but they are noise, not spend leakage. A few location anomalies confirm Shopify's filter is working: Falkenstein, Germany (home of the Hetzner server farm) shows a 50% add-to-cart rate; Montreal shows 5 sessions and 10 add-to-carts — bots re-adding to cart on loop. Shopify is correctly flagging them.

Why this matters

The bot share is real but it is not where money is being lost. It depresses the headline number; it is not the actionable problem. The actionable problem is the next finding.

Meta combined (Facebook + Instagram + fb) delivered 2,624 human sessions, 8 add-to-carts and 2 orders — a 0.30% ATC rate. Direct traffic delivered a comparable 2,908 sessions but 57 add-to-carts and 6 orders — four times the rate. Meta carried 90% of Direct's volume and produced 14% of its add-to-carts.

Add-to-cart rate by source vs. category benchmark
At 0.30% combined, Meta paid traffic runs at roughly one-twentieth of the luxury-fashion add-to-cart benchmark. Direct, even carrying the bot load, is four times stronger.

What this points to

A 0.30% add-to-cart rate on 2,600 real human sessions has a short list of likely causes — and Meta Ads Manager access would confirm which within 24 hours:

  • Loose audience targeting — interest stacks that aren't actually shaped like luxury-fashion buyers.
  • Worldwide or non-US-priority geo — international sessions on a US-only fulfilment brand.
  • A top-of-funnel optimisation event — Link Click, ViewContent or Reach instead of Add-to-Cart or Purchase, so the algorithm chases volume, not intent.
  • Audience Network placement — lower-quality humans from mis-tapped mobile interstitials.
  • Creative-audience mismatch — ads pitched casual, landing on a luxury site, bouncing on arrival.
The implication

Meta, as currently configured, is structurally inefficient at this budget. It does not need more spend — it needs to be rebuilt as a tight retargeting layer while the budget moves to channels where the buyer's intent exists before the click.

Finding 03

Direct traffic is the real engine — and it's under-monetised

Direct traffic produced 6 of 8 orders in the last 30 days. These are people who found Pecan Moon through word-of-mouth, organic Instagram, press, the founder's presence at events, or by typing the URL. They are the highest-intent audience the brand has — and almost nothing is built to capture or compound them.

What's missing on top of this audience

  • No abandoned-cart flow capturing the 45 checkouts that didn't convert
  • No browse-abandonment flow capturing 50+ add-to-carts that stalled
  • No retargeting layer — Meta catalog, Google, Pinterest — catching returning visitors
  • No email-capture optimised for Direct visitors, beyond a generic discount modal
  • No Branded Search campaign capturing the "Pecan Moon" queries these visitors generate
The opportunity hiding here

Pecan Moon's strongest channel is not instrumented to compound. Every dollar spent sending a low-intent session into the funnel is a dollar not spent capturing, retargeting and converting the high-intent audience that already arrives on its own.

Finding 04

The site itself converts

Two orders totalling $1,000 landed in the last week, against a prior baseline of zero completions. That matters more than it looks. It means the funnel is not structurally broken — the product closes when the right person arrives.

It also retires the most convenient excuse for April's underperformance — the idea that nobody buys luxury online without a long trust-building phase. The brand can close. The channel mix simply hasn't been delivering the right traffic.

Why this is the most important finding

It narrows the whole diagnosis to one question: why does paid traffic convert at a quarter the rate of organic? The answer isn't in the site. It's upstream — in who is being sent, and why.

Finding 05

The measurement layer is incomplete

Four gaps would have surfaced everything above far sooner. Closing them is a week-one priority:

  1. No GA4. The brand runs entirely on Shopify's native analytics — one source of truth, but without the multi-channel attribution depth to compare paid performance across platforms.
  2. No server-side tracking (CAPI). Post-iOS 14.5, the pixel alone undercounts conversions by 20–40% and feeds the algorithm a degraded signal.
  3. No post-purchase attribution survey. Nothing asks customers "how did you hear about us?" — the standard ground-truth check on paid attribution claims.
  4. No reconciliation between Meta and Shopify. The previous agency reported figures on the order of 100+ add-to-carts and 40+ initiated checkouts; Shopify recorded 23 and 18 for April. A gap that wide signals a pixel that is misfiring, double-firing, or being read off the wrong events.

The full attribution stack — server-side tracking, blended-ROAS reporting, a post-purchase survey and GA4 — costs roughly $375/month, paid directly to the vendors. It is inexpensive, and it is the infrastructure that lets us answer "did this work?" with data instead of platform-reported numbers.

First action — Meta Business Manager access

To turn the forensic inferences in Finding 02 into direct evidence, we need admin access to Pecan Moon's Meta Business Manager — a five-minute action that confirms the exact objectives, audiences, geo, placements, creative and spend behind April. We recommend requesting it now, regardless of which direction this proposal takes.

Part II
The Strategy

The analysis points to one move: redirect budget away from Meta prospecting toward channels where buyer intent already exists, instrument the funnel properly, and run Meta as a focused retargeting layer.

The opportunity

A category built for intent channels

Pecan Moon sits in one of the cleanest categories in DTC for intent-driven growth. Luxury resort wear is a planned, considered, visually-driven purchase — exactly the behaviour Google and Pinterest were built to harvest.

A $400–$1,200 vacation dress for a Mediterranean honeymoon is researched for weeks. Pinterest captures the planning phase, three to six months before the trip; Google captures the purchase moment — "Mykonos outfit," "Amalfi Coast dress." Meta is a scroll-interruption platform: best for scaling creative that is already proven, not for discovering what works.

The playbook here is not more Meta budget. It is claiming the destination-keyword whitespace on Google, owning Pinterest before competitors wake up to it, and running Meta as a tight retargeting layer until the audience and creative are validated. The rest of this section is the evidence for that call.

Beyond paid

Where this goes next

The 90-day plan above is the committed scope — paid channels, attribution, lifecycle and creative. But the most durable growth for a brand like Pecan Moon is earned, not bought. This is the roadmap beyond Phase 1: not in the current budget or hours, and noted here so the bigger picture is visible — we would scope it together once the paid engine is proven.

AI-search visibility (AEO / GEO)

When someone asks ChatGPT or Google's AI for "the best luxury resort wear brands," Pecan Moon currently has no presence in the answer. That is changeable: structured Schema markup, a library of destination-guide content ("What to wear in Mykonos"), and Pecan Moon's Shopify catalog surfacing in ChatGPT's organic shopping results. Unlike a paid click, an AI citation compounds — it keeps recommending the brand for years.

Earned media

The brands that win AI citations are the brands that win editorial citations. A PR and gifting program — hero pieces to editors at Vogue, WWD, Harper's Bazaar and Town & Country — authentic presence in the Reddit communities where destination-wedding and capsule-wardrobe buyers gather, a Wikipedia entity for the brand and founder, and targeted Substack placements together build a citation moat the comp set would need years to dismantle.

How to think about it

Treat this as Phase 2+ upside. The paid engine is what we prove first; the earned layer is the compounding bet we build once it is working — and it is a strong reason this brand's ceiling is well above what $3,000 of paid media alone implies.

Product identifiers & images

Pecan Moon is private-label: set identifier_exists = no, populate brand = Pecan Moon and a unique MPN per variant — never invent GTINs. Main image on a clean white background, product filling 75–90% of the frame, 1500×1500 or larger. Use all additional-image slots — on-model front and back, a fabric close-up, a styled flatlay, packaging, and lifestyle context. Products with both standard and lifestyle imagery see roughly 27% higher click-through.
