The honest read.

Demand is real. 25 women walked all the way to the price reveal. The funnel wasn't broken — it was never built.

0.93% add-to-cart is on pace for luxury launch (Statista 2025: 0.7–0.94%). The 4% checkout completion is off, but fixing checkout alone won't compound. Three things are leaking at once.

Three leaks, stacked.

1 · No email or SMS capture exists.

No welcome popup. No quiz. No newsletter form above the fold. Just a passive form in the footer that most visitors never see. Of the ~2,975 visits last month that left without buying, essentially zero gave permission to be contacted again. Every dollar of traffic — paid, organic, Instagram — is one-and-done. At Pecan Moon's price point ($300–$1,500+ per piece), the typical luxury buyer needs 15–20+ touchpoints across emails, ads, content, and brand exposure before they commit. Without an owned email list, none of those touchpoints can come from us after they leave the site.

2 · The site under-sells itself at every touchpoint.

Generic SEO tagline ("Luxury Resort Wear" — the line every competitor in the category could use verbatim). Free shipping at $1,000 (only triggers for buyers of the Angela poncho — invisible to anyone else). About page names Mary but doesn't yet hand her the microphone — no founder portrait, no signed letter, no Mediterranean-to-Austin origin on the page. Already working — don't touch: Shop Pay Installments render correctly on every product page ($495 Maria shows as "$123.75 × 4") — that four-payment option at the price-reveal moment is doing real work, and it's the one piece of the funnel that's already wired correctly.

3 · The category's biggest unclaimed opening is a founder-voice editorial — and that's where Mary fits.

Before writing this plan, we mapped twelve direct competitors in luxury resort wear at Pecan Moon's price tier — Zimmermann, Ulla Johnson, La DoubleJ, Cuyana, Khaite, Sézane, and others — looking specifically at how each one speaks to its customer through email and on-site copy.

The pattern that jumped out: only one of the twelve runs a true first-person founder voice. La DoubleJ's bi-weekly editorial — written by JJ Martin in her own voice, signed by her, opinionated, personal — is the only place in the entire category where the customer hears the founder directly. Zimmermann uses an anonymous brand voice. Ulla Johnson sends product drops with no editorial. The rest sit somewhere in the middle: clean, polished, and faceless.

That's the structural opening. Mary's Mediterranean-to-Austin story is a ready-made template for the same lane — and the Texan-Mediterranean register specifically isn't being occupied by anyone in the category. The 90-day plan that follows is, at its core, the system for putting Mary's voice into the buyer's inbox at the moments that matter.

The thesis: Mary's voice is the moat. Our job is the machinery that lets it scale. A "flow" is an automated email or SMS sequence triggered by a specific customer action — landing on the site, abandoning a cart, finishing a purchase. Klaviyo (the email/SMS platform we'll build on) tracks this across the entire apparel industry: flows generate 41% of total email revenue while making up only 5.3% of total sends. For a brand at Pecan Moon's stage — short list, high price point, scarce attention — that ratio is the most important number in the entire engagement. It's the lever we exist to pull.

Zapp ~5 hrs Week 5

Cart-abandon flow — 3 emails + optional SMS

Triggers when someone fills a cart and leaves without buying. The standard ecommerce cadence is 1hr / 24hr / 72hr; for a $300+ cart that's too aggressive — luxury buyers research and consult before committing. We slow it to 4hr / 36hr / 84hr instead. Email 1 is a quiet reminder. Email 2 adds styling context — what trip the piece is for, what climate it works in. Email 3 is the founder concierge: "Reply if Mary can help on fit." No discount in any of the three emails — at this price point a discount-led recovery permanently dilutes pricing power.

Industry average for apparel cart-abandon: 3.42% of recipients place an order, generating about $3.65 per recipient. The top 10% of brands hit 7.69%, generating $28.89 per recipient. Our target: 6–8% — meaning this single flow recovers 3–6 of the 24 near-buyers walking away every month.

Section 07 · Stop Doing

Five things to turn off, not on.

Most luxury direct-to-consumer playbooks recommend the inverse of what's below. The research is unambiguous that it's the wrong move at Pecan Moon's tier.

No "someone in [city] just bought this" pop-up notifications — Vitals or any other app.

The Vitals app is installed (and powering useful Wishlist + Smart Bar features). Its Live View / Sales Pop feature is currently off, which is the right setting. At one verified sale, any fake-name fake-city notification would read as fabricated — and that trick is associated with $19 dropshipping sites, not $325–$1,550 luxury. Lock it off and don't let any vendor or contractor turn it back on.

Never run a 10%-off welcome popup, even if capture pressure mounts.

The destination quiz outperforms a discount popup at every list size we found (Sleeknote: 8.65% vs 4.13%; Riddle: a ~11× lift). And at Pecan Moon's price point, a discount welcome permanently dilutes pricing power — the moment you train a buyer to wait for a sale, every full-price piece they consider becomes a question of how long before it goes on sale. Sézane's "no sales, no unsold stock" stance and Cucinelli's near-total absence of promotional codes both exist because the math at this tier is well-documented. Build the list with a quiz; never train the buyer to wait.

Stop leading with "Luxury Resort Wear" anywhere on-site.

It's the line currently set as your SEO meta description, and it's genuinely generic — every single competitor in the category could use the exact same line. Replace with named provenance: "Hand-finished knitwear, designed in Austin, made for the way you travel." Pair with Mary's voice rather than a category descriptor.

Don't display customer reviews on product pages until you have 10+ verified, photo-included reviews.

Below ~10 reviews, the on-site display actually hurts buyer trust at this price point — the research on this (Spiegel; Han/Nunes/Drèze 2010) is unambiguous. At zero reviews, it's worse. Until you cross the threshold, named-editor or stylist quotes do the work better than user reviews. The post-purchase Day 14 email manufactures the pipeline; we turn the widget on once you've got enough.

Stop running the $1,000 free-shipping threshold on a catalog where most pieces sit between $295–$595.

The current threshold only triggers for someone buying the Angela poncho. For everyone else, the promo bar advertises a benefit they can never actually access, which reads worse than no promo bar at all. Dropping it to $395 means the Maria, Olivia, Bella + accessories tier all qualify — exactly the price band we want to be growing into.

Section 08 · Mary's Moves

Five soft asks. Plan works without them — each one changes the trajectory.

Each is genuinely optional. Each is also more leverage than any paid channel for the same dollar.

Ask 01

One hero portrait of Mary — Austin atelier or at home.

Not a glossy shoot. One real photograph, used as hero on homepage or About. Every founder-led luxury brand in the cohort frames its founder this way.

Ask 02

A 60-second voice-note from Mary on why Pecan Moon exists.

We transcribe into Welcome flow Email 2. Most-read piece of email the brand will send anyone for the next year.

Ask 03

Mary signs cart-abandon E3 replies on $500+ carts.

"Hi, Mary here. Saw you saved the Maria. Holding it 24 hours, no pressure. Anything I can help with on fit?" Five replies a month, maybe ten by Day 90 — operationally feasible at this volume, and genuinely impossible to do at scale. Industry data from Postscript (one of the major SMS platforms) shows that two-way conversations with a sales associate see ~14× engagement and ~30% conversion lift over one-way blasts. The founder-as-associate version of that is the same mechanism with even more brand equity attached.

Ask 04

One Tuesday Edit per month written in Mary's voice.

200 words about what she's wearing or where she's going. We ghostwrite the other. Subject line + curation = ours. Voice once a month is what makes the Tuesday Edit a brand asset, not a product blast.

Ask 05

A handwritten birthday card to every first-year customer.

~30 cards Year 1, ~100 Year 2, ~300 Year 3. Cucinelli built a billion-dollar brand on this exact ritual. Pecan Moon at 30 cards is the original ritual.

The Moat

What we're actually building.

The 90 days end with: a list, a working machine, the start of a review base, a Tuesday cadence buyers anticipate, and a founder voice structurally differentiated in the category. Each is a compounding asset. None existed at the start.

The conservative case at Day 90: roughly 80 orders, $25–35K in revenue. The compounding case by Month 6: the same site converts 30–40 of the 2,800 monthly visits instead of 1, the email list crosses 5,000, the first reviews are surfacing on product pages, and the Tuesday Edit becomes a thing the right women in Austin and Charleston and Aspen wait for.

At the bottom of the luxury market, scale wins by spending more. At the top of emerging luxury, scale wins by doing the things that are physically impossible to do at scale.

Hand-finished knitwear in small runs. A founder named on every email. A signature reply on a $500 cart. A handwritten card on a birthday. A Tuesday at 10:00 AM that becomes a ritual. Each impossible at scale. That is the moat.

The 90 days build it. The next ten years defend it.
