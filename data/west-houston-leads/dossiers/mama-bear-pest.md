## 7. Vertical economics & ROI model

- **Ticket sizes (West Houston pest, typical ranges):** one-time general pest service ~$125-$250; **recurring quarterly plans ~$120-$175 per visit (~$400-$700/yr LTV+)**; rodent/exclusion jobs ~$300-$1,500; termite treatments ~$800-$2,500; mosquito seasonal packages ~$400-$700. Recurring plans are the prize -- they convert a one-off into annual recurring revenue.
- **Paid-search economics (this vertical, West Houston):** pest-control Google Ads CPCs run high (~$8-$25+/click on commercial-intent terms); blended cost-per-lead commonly ~$40-$120, and **cost-per-booked-job is dominated by the booking conversion rate** -- which is exactly the lever Zapp pulls. At a 7%-style form conversion, paid traffic is unaffordable; at a 25-30% booking rate, the same spend becomes profitable.
- **Seasonality:** strong spring-summer surge (mosquitoes, ants, wasps Apr-Sep) and a rodent push in fall/winter -- so a paid engine launched now (June) hits peak demand.
- **Royal-Pawz-style lift math (illustrative, their dollars):** Say a modest paid + GBP program drives **100 new-customer site visits/month**. At today's form-and-callback ~7-10% booking rate that's ~7-10 jobs. Lifting to a Royal-Pawz-style ~25-30% booking rate => **~25-30 jobs/month** from the same traffic. At a conservative **$200 blended first-job value**, that's a swing from ~$1,600 to ~$5,500/month -- **~$3,500-$4,000+/month in incremental revenue**, before counting recurring-plan LTV (each converted recurring customer is worth $400-$700+/yr). Even half that lift pays for the engine many times over. The point of leverage isn't more traffic first -- it's that *they're already throwing away most of the interested visitors they get.*
