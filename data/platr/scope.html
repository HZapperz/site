Performance Benchmark — why speed matters more than features

We ran Google Lighthouse audits on all three sites: Platr’s current site, My Fit Foods, and a custom Next.js site we built for another client. We tested twice — once on desktop, once on simulated mobile — because they tell very different stories.

How we tested
Tool Google Lighthouse v13.1 — the same engine behind PageSpeed Insights and the Core Web Vitals scores that directly affect search ranking.
Desktop test Modern computer, fast wired connection. No CPU or network throttling. This is what you see when you open any of these sites in Chrome right now.
Mobile test Simulated mid-tier phone (Moto G Power) on slow 4G — 1.6 Mbps, 150ms latency, 4× CPU slowdown. This is the exact test Google uses for search ranking.

All tests run April 13, 2026 against live production homepages. No caching advantages, no warm-up runs. Each site got the same conditions.

Desktop — fast WiFi, modern computer

On a desktop with good internet, all three sites are reasonably fast. Nobody’s bouncing because of load times here.

Why mobile performance matters
70%+ of Shopify traffic is mobile Most of your customers are on phones with varying connection quality — not MacBooks on fast WiFi. The mobile test reflects their real experience.
53% abandon after 3 seconds Google’s research: more than half of mobile visitors leave if a page takes longer than 3 seconds. Both Shopify sites are well past that threshold.
Speed directly affects search ranking Google uses the mobile Lighthouse scores (Core Web Vitals) as a ranking factor. A 41 hurts your organic visibility.
Every 100ms = 6.4% better checkout Google’s data: small speed gains compound through the entire funnel. Sites at 1 second convert at 3× the rate of 5-second sites (Portent).
The takeaway: On desktop, everyone looks fine. On mobile — where most of your customers are — the gap is massive. My Fit Foods has the right features but the wrong foundation. A custom Next.js build gives you MFF’s look and feel at 3× the mobile speed, because the architecture handles performance automatically instead of fighting the theme engine.

Your store is growing fast — the funnel isn’t keeping up

What the data is really telling us: Add-to-cart rate held steady at ~10% in both periods — the store opening didn’t change browsing behavior. The collapse was in Cart→Checkout and Checkout→Purchase. People are still browsing the menu online at the same rate, but many are now choosing to buy in-store instead of completing the online checkout.

This makes sense. Since the store opened, a significant portion of online traffic is people checking the menu before driving in. They add items to see pricing and build their order, but when they hit the Zapiet delivery gate — postal code, calendar picker, Sunday-only delivery — they realize it’s easier to just walk in. The online checkout doesn’t acknowledge that in-store is an option, so it loses these customers silently.

The honest take on conversion rates

We’re confident we can significantly improve online conversion, but getting back to the pre-store 6% rate may not be realistic — and that’s not necessarily a bad thing. Some portion of that drop is customers who now prefer buying in person, and that’s still revenue. The real opportunity is twofold:

Opportunity 1
Fix the online funnel for delivery customers

The Zapiet friction, missing urgency, and broken checkout flow are losing people who genuinely want weekly delivery. These are the fixes in this proposal — and they’re the most immediate revenue recovery.

Opportunity 2
Stop treating in-store browsers as lost conversions

Right now, the site has one flow: weekly Sunday delivery. A visitor checking the menu before driving in gets the same experience as someone ordering for delivery — including a confusing delivery step that makes no sense to them. These aren’t lost conversions; they’re a second audience with no path designed for them.

The two-flow solution

The site needs to recognize it serves two audiences with different intent. A simple UI solution: make it clear on the landing page that the online store is for weekly Sunday delivery, and offer a separate path for in-store visitors.

Order for Sunday Delivery

The weekly ordering flow — menu, deadline countdown, delivery scheduling, full checkout. Focused and clear about what it is: place your order by Friday for Sunday delivery. No confusion about when food arrives.

Visit Us In-Store

Today’s available menu, pricing, store hours, location. Optionally, an order-ahead or pickup option down the road. Gives in-store browsers a path that actually serves them instead of funneling them into a delivery flow they’ll abandon.

This reframes the conversion story. Instead of a 2.2% conversion rate with 97.8% “lost,” you’d have two intentional funnels — each measured against the right intent. The in-store browsers aren’t lost conversions; they’re an audience that currently has no path. Building one lets you measure the in-store contribution for the first time.

Note: All revenue projections in this document cover online sales only. The in-store halo effect — people browsing online, buying in person — is real revenue not captured in any of our funnel numbers. A better online experience lifts both channels. A two-flow approach would let us start measuring the in-store contribution for the first time.

Platr takes 18.7 seconds before visitors see main content. 53% of mobile visitors abandon after 3 seconds. MFF is even slower (21.8s) — 102 scripts per page. Our custom build: 6.1 seconds, 10ms blocking time. Copying MFF’s features without changing the platform means copying their speed problems.
A/B Testing
Test everything. Keep what wins. $0/mo.
Split traffic between two versions of any element — headlines, CTAs, card layouts, checkout flows. Measure with statistical significance. On Liquid, you ship and hope. On the custom platform, you compound.
Session Recording
Watch every hesitation and rage-click. $0/mo.
Built-in rrweb captures every session. On Liquid, you’d pay $300+/mo for Hotjar/FullStory — adding yet another script. Here it’s native, lighter, and you own the data.
Reactive Cart State
The reason half of Phase 3 features exist.
Liquid is server-rendered — adding an item reloads the page. In React, cart state is live: progress bars, subtotals, badges, savings tiers all update instantly. No reload, no flash, no lost scroll. Table stakes for modern e-commerce.
Replace Zapiet
Eliminate the #1 revenue leak entirely.
Phase 1 restyles Zapiet. Phase 3 replaces it. Purpose-built delivery flow: remembered preferences, clean date buttons, no postal code anxiety, native cart integration. Can’t replace it on Liquid — Zapiet controls its own rendering.
No App Conflicts
Every feature is owned code that works together.
Shopify apps inject their own CSS, JS, and DOM — they don’t coordinate. PageFly breaks when Loox loads. Klaviyo fires over AvisPlus. On the custom platform: no apps, no conflicts, no 2am breakage from vendor updates.
Future-Ready
20-hour features become 4-hour features.
Retainer hours go further. Seasonal campaigns are trivial. Multi-location ready. When the catalog grows from 15 to 50+, search/filter/sort just works. All data — recordings, tests, analytics — owned and exportable. No vendor lock-in.
Phase 1 fixes the friction. Phase 3 removes the ceiling.
On Liquid, you ship and hope. On the custom platform, you test, measure, iterate, and compound.
